# AI-Powered SEO Content Production Playbook

## 1. Overview
AI significantly accelerates content production and execution in SEO, enabling faster scaling and more efficient workflows. However, it does not replace the need for strategic thinking. Sustainable SEO success still depends on well-structured content systems, clear topical organization, and deliberate planning. Rather than acting as a standalone solution, AI functions best as a supporting tool within a broader strategy focused on building topical authority, maintaining content quality, and creating interconnected content ecosystems that align with search intent and user value.

## 2. SOP: Step-by-Step Process

### Step 1 — Build a Topical Map Before Writing a Single Article

Before generating any content with AI, you must establish a topical map that covers your niche comprehensively. AI-generated content without a semantic architecture will produce isolated articles that do not reinforce each other, a key ranking failure.
•	Identify your core topic (e.g. 'email marketing for SaaS')
•	Map all sub-topics, entity relationships, and query variants using a tool like Rankability or ChatGPT
•	Categorize topics into: pillar pages (broad), cluster pages (specific facets), and supporting FAQ pages
•	Prioritize coverage depth over volume — Google rewards topical completeness, not article count


(Source: - Koray Tugberk GUBUR — 'Importance of Topical Authority for SEO' | https://www.holisticseo.digital/theoretical-seo/topical-authority/ | 2021.
- Aleyda Solis — AI Search Optimization Roadmap, 'Use topical clusters: A comprehensive pillar page for main broader topics, and cluster pages around specific facets' | speakerdeck.com/aleyda | Published 2025)

---

### Step 2 — Identify Long-Tail Queries with High AI Overview Potential

With AI Overviews now dominant in Google SERPs, long-tail queries are the highest-value targets. They are more specific, easier for AI to serve, and less contested.
•	Use Google Search Console to find queries with impressions but low CTR (likely being answered by AI Overviews)
•	Use tools like AlsoAsked, AnswerThePublic, or Ahrefs' 'Questions' filter to find long-tail angles
•	Target conversational, multi-intent queries that require comprehensive answers — AI search rewards these


(Source: Aleyda Solis — 'Target and focus more on answering the long tail queries of your users. We are going to see a much bigger and more important impact from those as a consequence of the AI overviews' | majestic.com/seo-in-2025 | 2025)

---

### Step 3 — Conduct SERP & Competitor Content Audit

•	For each target keyword, analyze the top 10 ranking pages manually
•	Identify: what subtopics they cover, what they miss, what format they use (list, guide, table, FAQ)
•	Note E-E-A-T signals used: author bios, first-person experience, original data, quotes
•	Your AI-produced content must outperform these pages on depth, originality, and structure — not just length

(Source: Source: Nathan Gotch — 'Cover topics deeply but naturally — not by jamming keywords' | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=d2Bn5MFCFKs | 2025)

---

### Step 4 — Write a Detailed Brief Before Prompting AI

Never prompt AI cold. A strong brief is the difference between content that ranks and content that sounds generic. The brief should define:
•	Target keyword + secondary keywords
•	User intent (informational, transactional, navigational)
•	Target audience persona (who is reading this and why)
•	Mandatory subtopics to cover
•	Tone of voice (authoritative, conversational, technical)
•	Word count target and format (guide, comparison, FAQ, listicle)
•	E-E-A-T requirements: does this need first-person experience, expert quotes, original data?


(Source: Source: Aleyda Solis — '6 Elements of Effective AI Prompts for SEO: Who, What, Where, When, Why, How' | https://www.marketingpowerups.com/podcast/aleyda-solis-seo-ai-prompts/ | 2023)

---

### Step 5 — Use AI for Drafting, Structure, and Metadata (Not Final Copy)

Use AI (ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini) for the following specific tasks only — do not publish raw AI output:
•	Generate a full content outline based on your brief
•	Draft section-by-section content (prompt per section, not entire article at once)
•	Generate 5-10 title tag variations and select the best
•	Write meta descriptions for A/B testing
•	Identify semantic gaps by asking: 'What related topics am I missing?'
•	Suggest internal linking opportunities


(Source: Edwin Toonen — 'SEO in 2025: Experts reveal key trends and insights' | https://yoast.com/seo-in-2025 | 2024.
Aleyda Solis — 'Enhance your day-to-day tasks by leveraging AI' | https://majestic.com/seo-in-2024/aleyda-solis | 2024)

---

### Step 6 — Optimize Content for Chunk-Level Retrieval

AI search engines (Google AI Overviews, Perplexity, ChatGPT Search) do not rank pages — they retrieve the most relevant passages or 'chunks'. You must structure every section as a standalone, self-contained answer.
•	Write one idea per section — never cram multiple concepts into one paragraph
•	Start each H2/H3 section with a clear answer to the implied question
•	Use structured, accessible HTML with clear subheadings for every subtopic
•	Include a 'Key Takeaways' or 'Summary' box at the top of long articles
•	Keep passage language tight: avoid pronouns that rely on prior context (write as if each section is read alone)

(Source: Aleyda Solis — 'The AI Search Optimization Roadmap by Aleyda Solis' | https://speakerdeck.com/aleyda/the-ai-search-optimization-roadmap-by-aleyda-solis | 2025)

---

### Step 7 — Apply the Human Editing Checklist (Mandatory)

This step is non-negotiable. Raw AI content fails E-E-A-T standards and risks Google penalties. Every AI-produced piece must be edited by a human with subject expertise before publishing.
•	Check all facts — AI hallucinates statistics, dates, and citations
•	Add first-person experience where relevant ('In my tests…', 'When I implemented this…')
•	Insert original insights, opinions, or data not found in competitors' content
•	Replace generic AI phrasing with specific, concrete language
•	Verify that no claims are made without a source
•	Add author bio with credentials on every article


(Source: Lily Ray — 'SEO Frontrunner Lily Ray: "You Choose Your Own Adventure in SEO"' | https://seo.ai/blog/seo-frontrunner-lily-ray-you-choose-your-own-adventure-in-seo | 2024)

---

### Step 8 — Internal Linking & Semantic Structure

•	Every new article must link to at least 2-3 existing relevant articles on your site
•	Pillar pages must link to all relevant cluster pages — and vice versa
•	Use descriptive anchor text (not 'click here' — use the target keyword as anchor)
•	After publishing, update older articles to link back to the new one


(Source: Terrence Ngu — 'Entity-Based SEO: Building Topical Authority Through Semantic Links' | https://hashmeta.com/blog/entity-based-seo-building-topical-authority-through-semantic-links/ | 2025)

---

### Step 9 — Publish with Full Structured Data & Technical Foundations

•	Add Article schema (or FAQPage schema for FAQ content) to every piece
•	Ensure page speed: target under 3 seconds load time
•	Verify mobile rendering — Google indexes mobile-first
•	Add author Schema with sameAs links (LinkedIn, Twitter) to reinforce E-E-A-T
•	Submit to Google Search Console after publishing


(Source: Inside Engine — 'Google core updates: What they mean and how to recover' | https://searchengineland.com/guide/google-core-updates | 2025)

---

### Step 10 — Content Performance Review (Monthly)

•	Track rankings for target keywords in Google Search Console and Ahrefs/Semrush
•	Identify articles with declining impressions — these need content refreshes
•	Monitor AI Overview appearances: are your articles being cited in Google's AI answers?
•	Update articles with new data, examples, or expert quotes every 6-12 months
•	Prune or consolidate thin or underperforming content — do not let it dilute site quality

(Source: Source: Lily Ray — 'The Vicious Cycle of SEO: How We Got Here & Where We’re Going' | https://ipullrank.com/seo-week-2025-lily-ray | 2025)

## 3. Where Experts Disagree

### Disagreement 1: Volume vs. Quality — How Much AI Content Is Too Much?

Position A — Koray Tugberk GUBUR (Quality & Depth Over Volume)
Koray argues that topical authority requires comprehensive coverage of a topic ecosystem — but this does NOT mean publishing hundreds of AI-generated articles quickly. He emphasizes that each piece must be semantically rich and structured correctly. 'Topical Authority = Topical Coverage + Historical Data' — meaning you need both breadth and a track record of quality.

Position B — Programmatic SEO Advocates (Scale First)
A growing camp of SEOs argue that publishing large volumes of AI content — thousands of pages targeting long-tail keywords — can capture traffic quickly before competitors. Some GEO consultants have actively recommended: 'Use AI to generate thousands of new pages to increase your topical coverage and brand salience in AI search.'


My Position : I side with Koray and Lily Ray. Programmatic AI content at scale was briefly effective but Google's March 2024 core update — which reduced unhelpful content by 45% according to Google — has made mass AI publishing a significant risk. The evidence shows that quality, depth, and genuine topical authority beat volume. I recommend publishing fewer, better articles on a structured topical map.

---

### Disagreement 2: Build Authority First vs. Publish Widely First

Position A — Nathan Gotch (Authority Before Content Volume)
Nathan Gotch recommends that new websites build site authority before publishing large amounts of content. He advises creating 3-5 linkable assets first and spending 6 months promoting them aggressively before expanding into keyword-driven content. 'When you look across all the updates, very rarely do you see a DR 80 website getting hit.'
(Source: Nathan Gotch | adogy.com/seo-insights-from-nathan-gotch-2024 | 2024)

Position B — Aleyda Solis (Semantic Coverage in Parallel)
Aleyda argues that waiting on authority before publishing content is a missed opportunity. Instead, she recommends establishing topical clusters from the start, targeting long-tail queries that don't require high domain authority to rank, while building authority simultaneously through content promotion and UGC signals.

My Position: I lean toward Aleyda's approach with Nathan's caution applied. For small or new sites, publishing content without any authority signals is risky — but so is waiting 6 months to publish anything. My recommendation: publish 5-8 high-quality pillar articles on day one, simultaneously pursue 2-3 strong backlinks per month, and expand content volume only once domain rating reaches DR 20+.

## 4. What I Rejected and Why

### Rejected Idea 1: Generating Thousands of AI Pages for Topical Coverage

Several GEO guides and some SEO practitioners recommend mass-publishing AI-generated pages to capture as many long-tail queries as possible and signal broad topical authority to AI systems.
Why I rejected it: Lily Ray directly documented this as a 'risky GEO recommendation' that leads to algorithmic demotions and duplicate content nightmares. Google's March 2024 core update specifically targeted this tactic and reduced such content's visibility by 45%. The short-term traffic gains are heavily outweighed by the long-term penalty risk and brand reputation damage from publishing thin, automated content. Quality-first SEO consistently survives updates; volume-first SEO consistently gets hit.

(Source: Lily Ray — 'risky GEO recommendations...risk flooding the search index with low-quality, automated content that can lead to algorithmic demotions and penalties' | lilyraynyc.substack.com | January 19, 2026)

---

### Rejected Idea 2: Keyword Density as a Content Quality Signal

Some older SEO frameworks still recommend hitting keyword density targets (e.g. use the target keyword 1-2% of article length) as a core content optimization technique. Some basic AI SEO tools still surface this metric.
Why I rejected it: Aleyda Solis explicitly called out the absurdity of this when comparing traditional SEO to GEO: 'At least among the SEOs I know, no one had been optimizing based on keyword density in a long time.' Koray Tugberk GUBUR's entire methodology is built on entity and semantic relationships, not keyword frequency. Modern search engines use NLP and semantic understanding — they do not count how many times a word appears. Keyword density is a 2010 concept. Including it in a 2025 SOP would actively mislead practitioners.

## 5. My Original Ideas

### Original Idea: The 'Chunk-First Drafting' Method

None of the 10 experts I researched described a concrete writing workflow that starts from the chunk level and builds upward. Most workflow advice goes top-down: topic → outline → draft → edit. I propose inverting this for AI-assisted content production.
How It Works
Instead of asking AI to write a full article draft, you first identify the 5-8 most likely chunks that AI search engines would extract from your article (i.e., the specific questions a user might ask that your article should answer). You then write or prompt each chunk independently, optimize it as a standalone passage, and assemble the article from these self-contained units.
Why This Could Work
Aleyda Solis identified that AI search engines retrieve passages, not full pages — 'each chunk should be independently understandable without relying on the whole page for context.' If you design content chunk-first, every passage is automatically optimized for AI retrieval from the start, rather than retrofitted after the fact. This approach also forces writers to be specific and answer-oriented in every section, which improves human readability as a side effect.
How to Implement It
1.	List the 5-8 specific questions a user searching your topic would want answered
2.	For each question, write a 100-200 word standalone answer (the chunk)
3.	Validate each chunk: does it make sense without reading the rest of the article?
4.	Assemble chunks into a full article, adding transitions and context between them
5.	Add your pillar intro, conclusion, and E-E-A-T signals on top of the assembled chunks
This is testable: compare chunk-first articles vs. traditional drafts for AI Overview citation rates over 90 days.

## 6. Weaknesses of This Playbook

### Weakness 1 — AI Search Is Moving Faster Than Any SOP Can Track

Google's AI Mode, Perplexity, ChatGPT Search, and similar platforms are changing their retrieval and ranking mechanisms monthly. Recommendations that were valid in Q1 2025 may be outdated by Q3 2025. This playbook should be reviewed and updated every 6 months at minimum.

---

### Weakness 2 — Chunk-Level Optimization Is Not Proven at Scale

My original 'Chunk-First Drafting' method is theoretically grounded in how Aleyda Solis describes AI search retrieval, but I have not personally run controlled A/B tests on this approach. It could produce content that is choppy or disconnected if transitions are not handled carefully.

---

### Weakness 3 — The Playbook Assumes You Have Subject Matter Expertise

The human editing step (Step 7) requires someone with actual domain knowledge to add first-person experience, catch AI hallucinations, and add original insights. If your team lacks expertise in the content topic, this SOP will produce better-structured generic content — but still generic. The playbook cannot manufacture authority that does not exist.

---

### Weakness 4 — E-E-A-T Measurement Remains Opaque

Despite extensive guidance from Lily Ray and others on E-E-A-T best practices, Google has never published a precise formula for how it scores experience, expertise, authority, and trustworthiness. The recommendations in this playbook are based on expert interpretation of Google's public communications — not confirmed algorithmic factors.

---

### Weakness 5 — Results Timeline Is Uncertain

New content can take 3-12 months to rank in competitive niches. This playbook does not include a growth timeline or projections, because they would be speculative. Anyone expecting fast results from a content-first SEO strategy may become impatient and abandon the approach prematurely.
